The Intent

Nine days of jungle mist, rice paddy silences, and vegetarian exploration. This space is a live journal for my writing and photography journey from Singapore to the heart of Bali — not a travel guide, but a record of attention.

Day 01  ·  April 18, 2026

Arrival & Zen

📍 Bisma Street at Sunset

First Light in a Foreign Stillness

The flight from Changi was barely two and a half hours, but landing in Bali always feels like crossing a threshold — from the hum of productivity into something older and quieter. The guesthouse on Bisma Street revealed itself at golden hour, tucked behind a low stone wall and a curtain of bougainvillea.

The town was quieter than expected for April. Somewhere in the middle distance, a gamelan. No WiFi for the first hour, which turned out to be the best possible beginning.

Note to self: the vegetarian nasi goreng at the warung across from the rice field terrace is worth every rupiah. Order a second portion.
First Evening Mist

Day 02  ·  April 19, 2026

First Wander

📍 Ubud Market & Monkey Forest

The Market Before the Crowds

Seven in the morning and the Ubud market was already half-dismantled from its pre-dawn peak. The vendors who remained were the ones selling to locals — bundles of incense, towers of fruit offerings, loose-leaf tobacco in small paper twists. I bought nothing but photographed everything.

The Monkey Forest in the afternoon was a different kind of chaos — one long-tailed macaque stole my sunglasses within thirty seconds of entry, examined them with what I can only describe as contempt, and dropped them in a bush. Humbling.

Reminder that the forest has its own hierarchy, and you are very low in it.

Day 03  ·  April 20, 2026

Nature Flow

📍 Campuhan Ridge Walk, 6 AM

The Ridge Before the World Woke Up

Alarm at 5:15. The air at that hour is something else entirely — cool, dense with the smell of wet earth and frangipani, and utterly without other people. The Campuhan Ridge trail is narrow, flanked by alang-alang grass that brushes your arms as you walk toward the horizon.

Three pages of longhand notes completed before breakfast. Something about moving through physical space unlocks whatever stays blocked at a desk. The body thinks differently when it moves.

Photography lesson of the day: the best light lasted exactly nineteen minutes. Next time, arrive twenty minutes earlier and stay nineteen minutes later.

Day 04  ·  April 21, 2026

Sacred Ground

📍 Tirta Empul Temple

Water That Has Always Been Running

Tirta Empul means "holy water spring," and the water has been flowing through its stone spouts since the tenth century, give or take. The purification ritual — wading into the clear pools in a sarong, letting each spout run over you in sequence — felt less like religion and more like being allowed to exist simply, without performance.

Photographed the outer courtyard only, as felt right. Some places resist the lens. I put the camera away and just stood there for a while.

The light through the temple gate at 8 AM is the kind of thing you write a whole paragraph trying to describe, then delete because no words do it.

Day 05  ·  April 22, 2026

Slow Morning

📍 Village Warung & Writing Day

The Necessary Pause

There is a particular kind of doing-nothing that is not laziness but preparation. Today I chose it deliberately: coffee at the warung by six, notebook open for two hours, then a long walk with no destination, no camera, and no plan. The village lanes behind the main road are a different country entirely.

Found a small family compound with a courtyard garden where a woman was making ceremonial offerings. She looked up, smiled, and went back to her work. This is hospitality without performance.

Wrote 1,400 words today — more than any day this week. Rest, apparently, is productive.
The Lane with No Name

Day 06  ·  April 23, 2026

Artistic Drift

📍 Tegalalang Rice Terraces

The Architecture of Patience

Tegalalang is a reminder that the most beautiful things take generations to build. The paddies weren't designed — they accumulated, layer by layer, each carved by hand to catch water and light just so. Looking at them long enough, you start to see it as a living system, not a landscape.

Spent the morning doing compositional sketches. Perspective keeps shifting the further you descend. By the time I'd walked to the valley floor, the light overhead had turned fully golden.

Bought a small watercolor of the terraces from a local artist at the base. It cost less than a coffee in Singapore and will outlast most things I own.

Day 07  ·  April 24, 2026

Deep Forest

📍 Kanto Lampo Waterfall

Where the Noise Goes to Die

The path to Kanto Lampo drops steeply off a small road most tourists drive straight past. Forty steps down and the jungle closes behind you entirely. The waterfall isn't dramatic by any measure — it fans across a wide shelf of dark volcanic rock and falls in a thin silver curtain — but the sound it makes fills the entire space like a held chord.

I sat on the wet rock for an hour. A small offering — flowers, rice, a stick of incense — had been placed in a crevice nearby. Left absolutely alone by the forest.

No signal. Best hour of the trip so far.

─── Chapter VIII ───

Day 08  ·  April 25, 2026

Reflection

📍 Saraswati Water Palace

Still Water, Still Mind

The lotus flowers at Saraswati open slowly — not dramatically, just steadily. I sat at the edge of the reflecting pool for almost an hour. Nobody asked me to leave. Nobody asked me anything at all. This is, I think, the exact thing I came here for.

Tomorrow is departure day. The notebook is almost full. I don't think that's a coincidence — the place and the time conspired to make me write more honestly than I normally allow myself.

"To travel is to notice. To write is to notice that you noticed." — Something I scribbled between the second and third coffee, which may or may not be original.

─── Chapter IX ───

Day 09  ·  April 26, 2026

Departure

📍 Last Morning Light

The Grammar of Leaving

The last morning always arrives too quickly and too quietly. Woke before the alarm, made tea from the flask on the windowsill, and sat with the jungle for the final time. The mist was doing its usual thing — rising slowly through the canopy, turning the light a pale milky green — and I didn't photograph it. I just watched it.

Nine days is both too long and not nearly long enough. That's how you know it was right. The taxi came at ten. I left a small tip in the offering basket by the entrance, which felt correct even if it wasn't traditional. Ubud gave generously. Something should be given back.

Back to Singapore. Notebook: full. Notebook #2: ready.
